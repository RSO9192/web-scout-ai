Venice is already highly exposed because its flood problem is driven primarily by **relative** sea-level rise: the combination of global mean sea-level rise and local land subsidence. A 2021 review in *Natural Hazards and Earth System Sciences* states that the historic increase in Venice flood frequency since the mid-19th century is explained by relative mean sea-level rise, and that future relative sea-level rise will be the most important driver of increasing flood duration and intensity through this century, outweighing the small projected decrease in storminess. IPCC AR6 WGII uses Venice as a case study and summarizes the issue as one of rising relative sea level plus rapidly increasing flood frequency. On effectiveness, the same *Frontiers* study says MoSE remains effective at **protecting Venice from severe flooding during storm-surge events** in the 2050 scenarios it modeled, keeping maximum water levels in the city **below 1.2 m even in climate-change scenarios**. [Frontiers | Sea level and temperature extremes in a regulated Lagoon of Venice](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/climate/articles/10.3389/fclim.2023.1330388/full)

A more general MOSE-operability study by Umgiesser modeled sea-level rise from **0 to 200 cm** and found a sharper long-term limit. It concludes the barriers can keep lagoon water below the **110 cm safeguard level** **nearly all the time if sea-level rise does not exceed 50 cm**. The authors conclude Venice will likely continue facing flood risk despite MOSE, especially under partial failure and higher sea levels. [NHESS - Developing a framework for the assessment of current and future flood risk in Venice, Italy](https://nhess.copernicus.org/articles/22/2381/2022/nhess-22-2381-2022.html)

The study used **IPCC-based SSP1-2.6, SSP3-7.0, and SSP5-8.5** pathways together with measured subsidence to map flooding for **2050, 2100, and 2150**.
